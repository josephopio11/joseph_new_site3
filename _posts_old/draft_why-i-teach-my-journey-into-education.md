The question usually comes up during parent-teacher conferences or sometimes, in a moment of candid curiosity from a student. "Mr. Evans," they'll ask, "you were a software engineer, right? Why did you leave that to become a teacher?"

It’s a fair question. On the surface, it looks like a trade-down. I swapped stock options for staff meetings, agile sprints for semester plans, and a sleek, open-plan tech office for a classroom filled with the chaotic energy of teenagers. I left a world that builds the future to join a profession that, I’ve come to realize, builds the _futurists_.

And every time I’m asked, I smile. Because the answer isn’t a simple one-liner. It’s a story. It’s a journey from a world of logical certainties to one of beautiful, unpredictable human potential. This is that story.

## The Glow of the Monitor: Life Before the Classroom

For over a decade, my world was defined by the glow of a monitor. I was a software engineer at a fast-growing tech firm, and I loved it. Or, more accurately, I loved the _idea_ of it. I was a builder, a problem-solver. My days were a complex dance of algorithms, data structures, and debugging sessions that often stretched late into the night. There was a tangible satisfaction in writing a clean block of code, in watching a complex system flicker to life, in solving a puzzle that had stumped the entire team.

I remember one project in particular. We were building a recommendation engine, a labyrinth of machine learning and user data. I spent three straight weeks fueled by coffee and takeout, chasing a memory leak that was as elusive as it was catastrophic. When I finally found it—a single, misplaced pointer in thousands of lines of code—the relief was immense. My team celebrated, the project shipped, and for a moment, I felt like a hero.

But then, the feeling faded. The project was a success, but what had I really changed? A few metrics on a corporate dashboard ticked upwards. User engagement increased by a fraction of a percent. The world kept spinning. The code I had poured my life into was, ultimately, ephemeral. It would be refactored, replaced, and eventually become a forgotten relic in a legacy codebase. The human element felt distant, abstracted away behind layers of data and analytics. I was building things _for_ people, but I was never really connecting _with_ them.

The turning point wasn't a single, dramatic event. It was a slow-dawning realization, a quiet question that started to echo in the silence of my late-night coding sessions: _Is this it?_

## The First Line of a New Code: The Leap

The idea of teaching started as a whisper. I began mentoring junior developers at my company. I found myself more excited about explaining the elegance of a recursive function to a new hire than I was about my own projects. I loved watching their eyes light up when a difficult concept finally clicked into place. That "Aha!" moment was more rewarding than any successful code compilation.

I started volunteering, teaching a weekend coding class to middle schoolers at a local community center. The experience was terrifying, exhilarating, and utterly transformative. These weren't junior engineers; they were kids. They were messy, loud, and their questions were wild and wonderful. They didn't care about industry best practices; they wanted to know if they could code a game where a cat shoots lasers out of its eyes.

In that chaotic classroom, I found the human connection I had been missing. I wasn't just transferring information; I was igniting curiosity. I was giving them a new language to express their creativity. The code we wrote together wasn't for a corporate client; it was for them. It was a tool for their imagination.

The decision to leave my career was the scariest I’ve ever made. My friends thought I was crazy. My parents were supportive but concerned. I was walking away from a stable, lucrative, and respected profession to start over at the bottom of a new one. I enrolled in a teacher certification program, and suddenly, I was the student again. I learned about pedagogy, classroom management, and developmental psychology. I learned that teaching is not just about knowing your subject; it's about knowing your students. It’s a craft as complex and demanding as any software architecture.

My first year was a trial by fire. I made every mistake in the book. My lessons were too long, my explanations too technical, my expectations unrealistic. But amidst the failures, there were glimmers of success. A student who had been struggling with loops finally built a working program and beamed with pride. A group of girls who initially thought coding was "for boys" designed a brilliant app to help organize school events. These small victories felt more significant than any product launch I had ever been a part of.

## The Global Classroom: Finding My Place

That journey eventually led me here, to an international school. Teaching Computer Science in this environment is a unique privilege. My classroom is a microcosm of the world. On any given day, I have students from a dozen different countries, each bringing their own unique perspective, culture, and experience.

This diversity transforms the way we approach technology. When we discuss the ethics of AI, I’m not just talking to a monolithic group; I’m facilitating a conversation between a student from Seoul, another from São Paulo, and another from Stockholm. We don't just learn about data privacy in the abstract; we discuss how it's viewed differently in Europe, Asia, and North America.

My students are digital natives, but they are often passive consumers of technology rather than active creators. My job is to pull back the curtain. It’s to show them that the apps on their phones, the games they play, the websites they visit—these are not magic. They are built by people, with code, and they can be the ones to build the next generation of technology.

We do more than just learn Python or Java. We learn computational thinking—how to break down complex problems into manageable parts. We learn collaboration, debugging not just code but also our own misunderstandings. We learn resilience, because programming is a constant cycle of failure and iteration. These are not just skills for a tech career; they are skills for life.

## So, Why Do I Teach?

Which brings me back to the original question. Why did I trade my life as a coder for a life as a teacher?

I teach for the "Aha!" moments. The flicker of understanding in a student's eyes when a complex idea finally makes sense. It’s a moment of pure intellectual discovery, and I get to witness it every single day.

I teach because I am not just preparing students for a job. I am empowering them to be creators in a world that increasingly encourages consumption. I am giving them the tools to not just use the future, but to build it.

I teach because in a field that can feel cold and impersonal, the classroom is a place of profound human connection. It’s about building relationships, fostering confidence, and guiding young people as they figure out who they are and what they want to contribute to the world.

I left a job where I wrote code that would be obsolete in five years. Now, I get to write on the hearts and minds of the next generation. The logic I help them build, the problem-solving skills I help them hone, the ethical considerations I encourage them to ponder—that is a legacy that will not be refactored or deleted. It will be carried forward, iterated upon, and used to solve problems I can’t even imagine yet.

I am no longer just a software engineer. I am an architect of curiosity. A debugger of doubt. A compiler of confidence.

That is why I teach.
