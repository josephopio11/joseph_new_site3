## Debugging My Own Teaching: Reflecting on CS Instruction

As a Computer Science teacher in an international school, I often find myself drawing parallels between the world of coding and the world of education. Just as a program needs debugging to function optimally, so too does my teaching practice require constant reflection and refinement. This blog post is an exploration of that process – a journey into the challenges, triumphs, and ongoing evolution of my CS instruction.

### The Compiler's Eye: Initial Observations and Assumptions

When I first started teaching, I, like many educators, brought with me a set of preconceived notions about how students learn best. I assumed that a clear and concise lecture, followed by well-structured coding exercises, would be sufficient to impart the necessary knowledge and skills. I envisioned a classroom filled with eager learners, diligently typing away and mastering the intricacies of algorithms and data structures.

The reality, of course, was far more complex. Some students thrived under this approach, while others struggled to keep up. Some were naturally gifted programmers, while others found the abstract concepts of computer science daunting. It became clear that my initial assumptions were, at best, incomplete and, at worst, actively hindering the learning process for a significant portion of my students.

One of the earliest "bugs" I identified was the assumption that all students entered the classroom with the same level of prior knowledge. While some had experience with coding through extracurricular activities or personal projects, others were completely new to the field. This disparity created a significant challenge in terms of pacing and differentiation.

### Syntax Errors: Misunderstandings and Misconceptions

Beyond the issue of prior knowledge, I also encountered a variety of "syntax errors" in my students' understanding. These were fundamental misunderstandings of key concepts that prevented them from progressing further. For example, many students struggled with the concept of variables, confusing them with mathematical variables or simply failing to grasp their role in storing and manipulating data.

Another common misconception was the difference between assignment and equality. Students would often use the `=` operator incorrectly, leading to unexpected and frustrating results. These seemingly small errors could have a significant impact on their ability to write correct and efficient code.

Addressing these syntax errors required a shift in my teaching approach. I realized that I needed to spend more time explicitly explaining these fundamental concepts, using concrete examples and analogies to help students grasp their meaning. I also incorporated more interactive activities and formative assessments to identify and address misunderstandings early on.

### Runtime Errors: The Challenges of Active Learning

As I became more aware of the need for active learning, I began to incorporate more group projects, coding challenges, and peer instruction activities into my lessons. While these activities were generally well-received by students, they also presented new challenges.

One of the biggest "runtime errors" I encountered was the issue of group dynamics. Inevitably, some groups would be dominated by one or two students, while others would struggle to collaborate effectively. This could lead to uneven participation and a lack of learning for some students.

To address this issue, I implemented several strategies. I carefully assigned students to groups, taking into account their skills and personalities. I also provided clear guidelines for group work, emphasizing the importance of collaboration and shared responsibility. Furthermore, I incorporated individual accountability measures, such as individual reflections and quizzes, to ensure that all students were actively engaged in the learning process.

Another challenge was the management of classroom time. Active learning activities often took longer than I anticipated, leaving less time for direct instruction and individual support. To address this, I experimented with different time management techniques, such as using timers, breaking down activities into smaller chunks, and providing students with clear expectations for each task.

### Logic Errors: Rethinking Assessment Strategies

Traditional assessment methods, such as exams and quizzes, often focus on rote memorization and the ability to apply algorithms to specific problems. While these skills are important, they don't necessarily reflect a student's ability to think critically, solve complex problems, and collaborate effectively – all of which are essential skills for success in the field of computer science.

I realized that I needed to incorporate more authentic assessment tasks that would allow students to demonstrate their understanding in a more meaningful way. This led me to experiment with project-based assessments, coding portfolios, and peer reviews.

Project-based assessments allowed students to apply their knowledge and skills to solve real-world problems. Coding portfolios provided a platform for students to showcase their best work and reflect on their learning journey. Peer reviews allowed students to provide constructive feedback to their peers, fostering a culture of collaboration and continuous improvement.

These alternative assessment methods provided a more holistic view of student learning, allowing me to identify areas where students excelled and areas where they needed additional support. They also encouraged students to take ownership of their learning and develop a deeper understanding of the subject matter.

### Optimization: Leveraging Technology and Resources

In today's digital age, there is a wealth of technology and resources available to support computer science education. From online coding platforms to interactive simulations, these tools can enhance the learning experience and make complex concepts more accessible.

I have experimented with a variety of these tools in my classroom, including:

- **Online Coding Platforms:** Platforms like Codecademy, Khan Academy, and freeCodeCamp provide students with interactive coding exercises and tutorials.
- **Integrated Development Environments (IDEs):** IDEs like VS Code and Repl.it provide students with a powerful environment for writing, debugging, and running code.
- **Educational Robotics Kits:** Robotics kits like LEGO Mindstorms and VEX Robotics allow students to apply their coding skills to build and program robots.
- **Gamified Learning Platforms:** Platforms like CodeCombat and Scratch engage students in coding through games and interactive challenges.

While these tools can be incredibly valuable, it's important to use them strategically and purposefully. Simply introducing technology into the classroom is not enough. It's essential to carefully consider how these tools can be used to enhance the learning experience and achieve specific learning objectives.

### Continuous Integration: The Importance of Feedback and Reflection

Debugging my teaching is not a one-time event, but rather an ongoing process of reflection and refinement. It requires a willingness to experiment with new approaches, gather feedback from students and colleagues, and continuously adapt my teaching to meet the evolving needs of my students.

One of the most valuable sources of feedback is my students themselves. I regularly solicit their feedback through surveys, informal discussions, and anonymous feedback forms. This feedback provides valuable insights into what is working well and what needs improvement.

I also collaborate with my colleagues to share best practices and learn from their experiences. We regularly observe each other's lessons, provide constructive feedback, and engage in professional development activities.

Through this continuous process of feedback and reflection, I am able to identify and address the "bugs" in my teaching, optimize my instruction, and create a more engaging and effective learning environment for my students.

### The Final Compilation: A Teacher's Journey

Debugging my own teaching is a challenging but ultimately rewarding endeavor. It requires a willingness to be vulnerable, to admit mistakes, and to continuously strive for improvement. As I continue on this journey, I am reminded of the words of the great computer scientist, Donald Knuth: "Programming is not about typing code, it's about thinking." Similarly, teaching is not just about delivering content, it's about fostering a love of learning and empowering students to become critical thinkers and problem solvers. And just like any complex program, the process of becoming an effective teacher is a journey of continuous debugging, refinement, and optimization. The "program" of my teaching is never truly finished, but that's what makes it so exciting.
